# Provenance Guard

A backend API system for classifying whether submitted creative text was written by a human or generated by AI. Built for creative sharing platforms to provide transparent attribution labels, honest confidence scoring, and a fair appeals process for contested classifications.

---

## Architecture

A submitted piece of text flows through two independent detection signals before scoring, labeling, and logging happen. Each signal is computed independently so either can be debugged or replaced in isolation.

```
POST /submit {text, creator_id}
        |
        v
  [Flask: generate content_id]
        |
        v
  [Signal 1: Groq LLM] ---------> llm_score (0-1)
        |
        v
  [Signal 2: Stylometric heuristics] --> stylometric_score (0-1)
        |
        v
  [Confidence Scoring: 0.6*llm + 0.4*stylo] --> combined_confidence
        |
        v
  [Label Generator: map score to label text]
        |
        v
  [Audit Log: write structured JSON entry]
        |
        v
  Response: {content_id, attribution, confidence, label}


POST /appeal {content_id, creator_reasoning}
        |
        v
  [Flask: look up content_id in log]
        |
        v
  [Update status -> "under_review"]
        |
        v
  [Audit Log: write appeal entry with original signal scores]
        |
        v
  Response: {confirmation, content_id, status}
```

**System components:**
- `app.py` — Flask routes, confidence scoring logic, label generation
- `signals.py` — Signal 1 (Groq LLM) and Signal 2 (stylometric heuristics)
- `storage.py` — JSON-based audit log (read, write, lookup by content_id)
- `audit_log.json` — structured log file written at runtime (not committed; see sample below)

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1 — LLM Judgment (Groq, llama-3.3-70b-versatile)
**What it measures:** holistic semantic and stylistic coherence. We prompt the model to assess whether text reads as AI-generated or human-written based on sentence rhythm, word choice specificity, presence of genuine idiosyncrasy, and overall naturalness.

**Output:** float 0–1, where 1.0 = strongly AI-generated, 0.0 = strongly human-written.

**Why it works:** AI text tends toward generic phrasing, uniform rhythm, and absence of the small imperfections and specificity that characterize human writing. The LLM can detect these holistically in a way that pure statistics cannot.

**Blind spot:** it is a black box — we cannot inspect why it gave a particular score. It can be fooled by short texts, heavily edited AI output, or very formal human writing that happens to sound generic.

**Verified behavior (tested standalone before wiring into Flask):**
- Casual human text ("ok so i finally tried that ramen place..."): **score 0.2**
- Formulaic AI-style text ("Artificial intelligence represents a transformative paradigm shift..."): **score 0.8**

### Signal 2 — Stylometric Heuristics (pure Python, no external libraries)
**What it measures:** structural/statistical properties of the text — sentence length variance and type-token ratio (vocabulary diversity). AI text tends to be more uniform across these metrics; human writing is more variable.

**Output:** float 0–1, where 1.0 = high uniformity (AI-like), 0.0 = high variability (human-like).

**Sub-metrics:**
- Sentence length standard deviation — low std dev (uniform sentences) → higher AI-likeness. Normalized against a calibrated range: std dev of ~0 is maximally uniform, std dev of ~6+ words is highly variable.
- Type-token ratio — unique words / total words, normalized to the 0.7–1.0 range (short texts almost always have high TTR, so the full 0–1 range is not meaningful here).
- Combined internally as: `(0.6 × variance_score) + (0.4 × ttr_score)`

**Why it works:** structural uniformity is a real correlate of AI-generated text — models tend to produce consistent sentence rhythms and somewhat generic vocabulary. This signal is independent of the LLM signal (structural vs. semantic), so when they agree it strengthens confidence and when they disagree it produces an honest "uncertain" result.

**Blind spot:** uniformity is a correlate, not proof. A minimalist human writer, a children's poem, or a non-native speaker writing carefully can all produce uniform structure without being AI-generated. This is the primary false-positive risk and is documented in Known Limitations below.

**Why two signals:** one is semantic (holistic LLM judgment), one is structural (measurable statistics). They are genuinely independent — a text can fool one without fooling the other, which is why combining them is more informative than either alone.

## Confidence Scoring

**Formula:** `combined_confidence = (0.6 × llm_score) + (0.4 × stylometric_score)`

The LLM signal is weighted higher (0.6) because it is more semantically aware. The stylometric signal (0.4) still pulls the combined score meaningfully when the two signals disagree — this is what produces "uncertain" results rather than always deferring to the LLM.

**Thresholds (from planning.md, intentionally asymmetric):**

| Confidence Range | Attribution | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| ≥ 0.75 | `likely_ai` | Requires strong agreement from both signals |
| 0.40 – 0.74 | `uncertain` | Wide band captures genuine ambiguity honestly |
| < 0.40 | `likely_human` | Low bar to clear — favors creators |

**Why asymmetric thresholds:** a false positive (calling a human's work AI-generated) is worse than a false negative on a creative platform. The "likely_ai" band starts at 0.75 — well above 0.5 — so the system requires meaningful signal strength before asserting AI authorship. The "uncertain" band is deliberately wide (0.40–0.74) to catch ambiguous cases rather than forcing a binary call.

**Example submissions showing meaningful score variation:**

| Input | LLM Score | Stylometric Score | Combined | Label |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Casual ramen review (human) | 0.2 | 0.228 | **0.211** | likely_human |
| Formal AI-style text | 0.8 | 0.187 | **0.555** | uncertain |
| Borderline finance writing | 0.7 | 0.374 | **0.570** | uncertain |
| Formulaic buzzword text | 0.9 | 0.625 | **0.790** | likely_ai |

A score of 0.51 and a score of 0.95 produce meaningfully different labels — the former lands in "uncertain," the latter in "likely_ai." The system does not binary-flip at 0.5.

## Transparency Labels

Three label variants are implemented. All three are reachable and were verified during testing. The exact text shown to users:

| Variant | Confidence Range | Label Text |
|---|---|---|
| High-confidence AI | ≥ 0.75 | "This content shows strong signals of AI generation. Our system is fairly confident this was AI-written, but no detection method is perfect — if you believe this is incorrect, you can appeal this classification." |
| Uncertain | 0.40 – 0.74 | "We're not confident either way about this content's origin. The signals we use gave mixed or weak indicators. This label reflects genuine uncertainty, not a hidden verdict." |
| High-confidence Human | < 0.40 | "This content shows strong signals of human authorship. Our system found no significant indicators of AI generation." |

## Appeals Workflow

**Endpoint:** `POST /appeal`

**Who can appeal:** any creator with the `content_id` returned at submission time.

**What they provide:**
```json
{
  "content_id": "f5bd547e-28cb-4e84-b68d-b4faa4bb8c7a",
  "creator_reasoning": "I wrote this myself from personal experience..."
}
```

**What the system does:**
1. Looks up the original classified entry by `content_id`
2. Updates status to `under_review`
3. Writes a new audit log entry containing the appeal reasoning plus the original signal scores and confidence — so a human reviewer has full context without needing to join multiple records
4. Returns confirmation to the creator

**Automated re-classification:** not implemented by design. The system acknowledges that no automated detector is reliable enough to adjudicate its own appeals — that requires a human reviewer.

**What a reviewer sees** in the audit log:
```json
{
  "content_id": "f5bd547e-28cb-4e84-b68d-b4faa4bb8c7a",
  "status": "under_review",
  "appeal_reasoning": "I wrote this myself from personal experience. I am a non-native English speaker and my writing style may appear more formal than typical human writing.",
  "original_attribution": "likely_human",
  "original_confidence": 0.211,
  "original_llm_score": 0.2,
  "original_stylometric_score": 0.228,
  "timestamp": "2026-07-01T05:16:30.047354+00:00"
}
```

**Test the appeal endpoint:**
```bash
curl -s -X POST http://localhost:5050/appeal \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{"content_id": "YOUR-CONTENT-ID", "creator_reasoning": "Your reasoning here"}' \
  | python3 -m json.tool
```

## Rate Limiting

**Limits:** 10 requests per minute, 100 requests per day per IP address (applied to `POST /submit` only).

**Reasoning:**
- A legitimate creator submitting their own work would rarely submit more than a few pieces per session — 10/minute is generous for real usage.
- 100/day covers a highly active user while making automated flooding expensive.
- The `/appeal` and `/log` endpoints are not rate-limited — appeals should never be artificially blocked, and log access is read-only.

**Verified behavior** (12 rapid requests, exceeding the 10/minute limit):

## API Reference

### POST /submit
Accepts a piece of text for attribution analysis.

**Request:**
```json
{
  "text": "Your content here",
  "creator_id": "unique-creator-identifier"
}
```

**Response:**
```json
{
  "content_id": "uuid-string",
  "attribution": "likely_human | uncertain | likely_ai",
  "confidence": 0.211,
  "label": "Transparency label text shown to users"
}
```

### POST /appeal
Contest a classification result.

**Request:**
```json
{
  "content_id": "uuid-from-submit-response",
  "creator_reasoning": "Why you believe the classification is incorrect"
}
```

**Response:**
```json
{
  "message": "Appeal received. Your content has been flagged for human review.",
  "content_id": "uuid-string",
  "status": "under_review"
}
```

### GET /log
Returns the most recent audit log entries (newest first).

## Known Limitations

**1. Short or stylistically uniform human writing will score as AI-like on the stylometric signal.**
The sentence length variance and TTR metrics measure uniformity, not AI authorship directly. A minimalist poem, a children's story using deliberate repetition, or a non-native English speaker writing carefully in a formal register can all produce low sentence variance — exactly what the stylometric signal flags as AI-like. This is documented as a known false-positive risk. The wide "uncertain" band (0.40–0.74) exists specifically to absorb these cases rather than mislabeling them as AI-generated.

**2. The stylometric signal is unreliable on very short texts (fewer than 2 sentences).**
Sentence length variance is mathematically undefined or meaningless with one sentence. The system explicitly returns a score of 0.5 (uncertain) for single-sentence inputs rather than a misleadingly confident number. Short creative forms like haiku or one-line captions will always produce uncertain results from the stylometric signal.

**3. The confidence scoring uses a fixed weighted average with hand-tuned thresholds.**
The 0.6/0.4 weighting and the 0.75/0.40 thresholds are calibrated heuristics, not parameters derived from a labeled training dataset. A deployed production system would learn these weights from ground-truth human/AI labeled data and use proper calibration curves rather than a linear combination.

## Spec Reflection

**Where the spec helped:** The planning.md requirement to write out the three exact label variants before building forced a real UX decision upfront — specifically the choice to make the "uncertain" label explicitly say "this label reflects genuine uncertainty, not a hidden verdict." Without that spec step, the uncertain label would likely have been vague filler text rather than something that actively reassures a creator that the system isn't hiding a verdict from them.

**Where implementation diverged:** The stylometric signal's TTR normalization range had to be recalibrated during testing. The spec assumed a TTR range of 0.4–0.8 would be meaningful for short texts, but in practice short texts almost universally have high TTR (0.85+), making that range useless. The fix was shifting the normalization window to 0.7–1.0 — a change discovered only by running real test inputs and seeing both texts score near zero on the TTR component, which is exactly why the spec instructs you to test signals independently before wiring them in.
